The Core Distinction

Why & How.
Two different questions.

Australia's AI governance answers two fundamentally different questions. The 8 Ethics Principles answer why — they are the foundational values that AI must uphold, published in 2019 and still the bedrock of everything.

The 6 AI Essential Practices (AI6) answer how — they are the operational playbook for implementing those values, released in October 2025 to replace the old 10-guardrail VAISS.

They are complementary, not competing. AI6 was explicitly built on top of the 8 principles. Every practice maps back to one or more principles.

What happened to the 10 guardrails?
The Voluntary AI Safety Standard (VAISS) had 10 guardrails (2024). NAIC condensed and strengthened them into 6 practices in October 2025. All 10 are still present — repackaged inside the 6. If your org was aligned to VAISS, you're not starting over.

Common Questions

Frequently asked

Are the 8 Ethics Principles still legally relevant if AI6 replaced VAISS?
Yes — absolutely. The 8 Ethics Principles were never superseded. AI6 (released October 2025) explicitly builds upon them. Every AI6 practice maps back to one or more of the 8 principles. The DTA's AI Technical Standard (TSGUAI, July 2025), the Policy for Responsible Use of AI in Government v2.0, and the APS AI Plan 2025 all reference the 8 principles as the foundational layer. If you are assessed for AI governance compliance, you will be assessed against the 8 principles — AI6 is the mechanism for satisfying them.
Is AI6 mandatory or voluntary?
AI6 is currently voluntary — but it is the de facto enterprise standard. Law firm MinterEllison (November 2025) noted it "will likely become industry best practice." The ACCC, OAIC and sector regulators reference AI6 alignment when assessing whether an organisation acted responsibly. For government agencies, the mandatory Policy v2.0 and TSGUAI incorporate AI6 principles operationally. For the private sector, alignment with AI6 is the clearest way to demonstrate responsible AI governance to regulators, boards and auditors. Treat it as mandatory in practice.
If my organisation was aligned to VAISS, do I need to start over?
No. NAIC published an official VAISS × AI6 crosswalk showing how all 10 guardrails map directly into the 6 practices. The 10 guardrails are retained as detailed control statements within the AI6 Implementation Practices document. Download it from industry.gov.au/publications/guidance-for-ai-adoption. Your existing controls remain valid — you simply need to reframe them within the AI6 structure and fill any gaps (particularly around the TSGUAI lifecycle stages and the December 2026 Privacy Act automated decision-making obligations).
Which principle covers the Privacy Act APP 1.7 automated decision-making obligation?
Principle 6 — Transparency & Explainability is the primary ethics anchor. People must understand when AI significantly impacts them. The incoming APP 1.7–1.9 obligations (mandatory from 10 December 2026) operationalise this principle by requiring all APP entities to disclose in their privacy policy: the types of personal information used in substantially automated decisions, and the nature of decisions that could significantly affect individual rights. This is also covered by AI6 Practice 4 — Share Essential Information. Begin privacy policy updates now — the obligation applies to all decisions made from that date, regardless of when the AI system was deployed.
Does Principle 7 (Contestability) require a formal appeal process?
In practical terms, yes for high-risk use cases. Principle 7 states that a "timely process" must allow people to challenge AI use or outcomes when they are significantly affected. AI6 Practice 6 (Maintain Human Control) operationalises this by requiring challenge and appeal pathways to be designed before deployment. Privacy Act Tranche 2 reforms (expected post-2027) are expected to formalise a statutory right to request meaningful information about automated decisions. Don't wait — build contestability into your AI systems now as leading practice. Document the appeal pathway in your AI register.
Do the 8 Principles apply to AI systems built before they were published?
The 8 Principles themselves are aspirational guidance, not retrospective law. However, the legal obligations that the principles connect to — Privacy Act APPs, Australian Consumer Law, anti-discrimination legislation — apply to all active AI systems regardless of when they were built. And from 10 December 2026, the Privacy Act's APP 1.7 automated decision-making disclosure obligations apply to all decisions made from that date, even if the underlying AI system was deployed years earlier. In practice, you should assess all active AI systems against the 8 principles and AI6 practices, including legacy systems.
How do the 8 Principles relate to the DTA AI Technical Standard (TSGUAI)?
The TSGUAI (released July 2025) is the most detailed technical governance document for AI in Australia. Its 42 statements across 3 phases (Discover, Operate, Retire) and 8 stages are all grounded in the 8 Ethics Principles. Specifically: the Design stage requires an ethics workshop aligning AI to the principles; the Data stage requires bias management (Principle 3 — Fairness); the Monitoring stage requires continuous performance tracking (Principle 5 — Reliability & Safety); and the Decommission stage requires responsible retirement (Principle 1 — Wellbeing). The TSGUAI is the technical implementation layer sitting beneath AI6. Source: digital.gov.au/policy/ai/AI-technical-standard.
