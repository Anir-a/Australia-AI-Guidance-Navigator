The Core Distinction

Why & How.
Two different questions.

Australian AI guidance commonly answers two different questions. The 8 Ethics Principles answer why — they describe the responsible AI values published in 2019.

The 6 AI Essential Practices (AI6) answer how — they provide practical steps for responsible AI governance and adoption, published in October 2025 as an update to VAISS.

They are complementary, not competing. AI6 maintains alignment with the 8 principles, so the page shows them together instead of treating them as separate documents.

May 2026 Update — Agentic AI

Agentic AI is the new risk layer

This is not presented here as a separate governance framework. It is a security and control lens for AI systems that can reason, plan, use tools, access data, call APIs and act with some autonomy. Use it to strengthen the 8 Principles and AI6 practices already shown on this page.

🛑

Source update · 1 May 2026: ASD's ACSC, CISA, NSA, the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security, NCSC-NZ and NCSC-UK published a joint source document called Careful adoption of agentic AI services.

The practical message for this navigator: start with low-risk and non-sensitive tasks, avoid broad access, and design for identity, least privilege, monitoring, auditability, human oversight and rollback.

Normal GenAI

Creates content for humans

Standard generative AI mainly produces text, images, summaries, drafts or analysis. A human usually decides what to do next.

  • Lower autonomy by default
  • Usually limited to advisory output
  • Main risks: privacy, hallucination, bias, data leakage and weak review
Agentic AI

Can take action through tools

Agentic AI builds on GenAI by integrating with software systems so it can reason, plan and take actions without continuous human intervention.

  • Higher autonomy and operational impact
  • Can call APIs, use tools, access memory and trigger workflows
  • Main risks: excessive privilege, prompt injection, tool misuse, cascading failure, weak accountability
🔐
Privilege risk
Over-permissioned agents can act beyond intended scope or be misused as a trusted deputy.
🧭
Behaviour risk
Agents may misread goals, over-optimise, chain unsafe actions or behave unpredictably.
🧩
Complexity risk
Tools, memory, data sources, sub-agents and handoffs expand the attack surface.
🧾
Accountability risk
Long, distributed agent chains can make it hard to explain who or what caused an outcome.

Agent-safe adoption checklist

Use this as the simple enterprise pattern: do not block innovation, but do not allow agents to become invisible privileged users. Every agent should have a named owner, bounded purpose, limited permissions, logging, monitoring, human approval points and rollback.

Why this works better

It shows governance as a journey, not a wall of controls. The reader can follow the train from idea to retirement.

  • Station 1–3: prove value, ownership and risk.
  • Station 4–6: control access, test hard and approve safely.
  • Station 7–8: monitor, improve, retire or redesign.

Agent-specific gates

For agents, the riskiest part is not the chat response. It is what the agent can access and do.

  • Can it call tools, APIs or workflows?
  • Does it have more access than it needs?
  • Are tool calls, memory and actions logged?
  • Can a human pause, override or shut it down?
  • Is there a rollback path if it acts incorrectly?

Common Questions

Frequently asked

Are the 8 Ethics Principles still useful if AI6 updated VAISS?
Yes. The 8 Ethics Principles are still a useful values layer. The October 2025 Guidance for AI Adoption says the 6 practices maintain alignment with Australia’s AI Ethics Principles and international standards. This page shows both layers together so readers can move from values to practical controls without jumping between documents.
How should I treat AI6?
For general business use, the Guidance for AI Adoption is presented as practical guidance for responsible AI adoption. Government agencies and regulated sectors may also have separate policy, privacy, cyber, procurement or sector-specific obligations. Use AI6 as a clear operating model, but check the source pages and your organisation’s legal/compliance requirements before treating it as a binding requirement.
If my organisation used VAISS, do I need to start over?
No, not from zero. The October 2025 update condensed the 10 VAISS guardrails into 6 essential practices. Use the 6-practice view to simplify your current register, controls and review process. Then check the source documents for any detailed control gaps, especially around risk, monitoring, transparency and human control.
Which principle covers the Privacy Act APP 1.7 automated decision-making obligation?
Principle 6 — Transparency & Explainability is the primary ethics anchor. People must understand when AI significantly impacts them. The incoming APP 1.7–1.9 privacy policy obligations (from 10 December 2026) operationalise this principle by requiring all APP entities to disclose in their privacy policy: the types of personal information used in substantially automated decisions, and the nature of decisions that could significantly affect individual rights. This is also covered by AI6 Practice 4 — Share Essential Information. Begin privacy policy updates now — the obligation applies to all decisions made from that date, regardless of when the AI system was deployed.
Does Principle 7 (Contestability) require a formal appeal process?
In practical terms, yes for high-risk use cases. Principle 7 states that a "timely process" must allow people to challenge AI use or outcomes when they are significantly affected. AI6 Practice 6 (Maintain Human Control) operationalises this by requiring challenge and appeal pathways to be designed before deployment. Privacy Act Tranche 2 reforms (expected post-2027) are expected to formalise a statutory right to request meaningful information about automated decisions. Don't wait — build contestability into your AI systems now as leading practice. Document the appeal pathway in your AI register.
Should older AI systems be reviewed?
Yes. Even if a system was built earlier, active AI use should be reviewed against current governance, privacy, cyber and business risk expectations. For privacy, OAIC guidance says APP entities will have extra privacy policy obligations from 10 December 2026 where personal information is used by a computer program for decisions that could significantly affect an individual’s rights or interests.
Where does the 1 May 2026 agentic AI guidance fit?
It sits as a security and operational risk layer on top of the existing framework. The 8 Principles still explain the values, AI6 still explains the responsible AI operating model, and the ACSC agentic AI guidance explains what changes when an AI system can use tools, access data, call APIs, create sub-tasks or act without continuous human intervention. In practice, agentic AI needs stronger identity, least privilege, monitoring, logging, human approval points, safe defaults, isolation and rollback.
How does this relate to the DTA AI Technical Standard?
The DTA AI Technical Standard is a separate source for government AI delivery and technical governance. This page does not replace that standard. It simply helps readers understand the relationship between values, adoption practices and the newer agentic AI control lens. Source: digital.gov.au/policy/ai/AI-technical-standard.
