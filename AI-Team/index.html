01 · Why this matters

AI is becoming employee infrastructure

The gap is not business AI strategy. The gap is Technology ownership of the AI operating layer that employees use every day.

Fact-based · no inflated claims
Why now
1
AI use is now mainstreamStanford HAI’s AI Index tracks rapid growth in AI capability, investment and organisational use.
2
Copilot needs secure data readinessMicrosoft guidance focuses on oversharing remediation, guardrails and AI-related compliance obligations.
3
AI risk needs a management systemNIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001 support structured governance, controls and continual improvement.
4
AI assurance is becoming expectedAustralian public-sector guidance points to accountable AI use, assurance practices, training and registers.
5
Agents and MCP increase integration control needsMCP connects AI apps to external systems, tools and workflows. That makes access and connector control important.
If nobody owns it
!
Shadow AI and tool sprawlTeams adopt tools, plugins or agents without visibility or control.
!
Overshared data and weak permissionsAI surfaces what users can access, so poor permissions become a bigger exposure.
!
Poor employee experienceEmployees get mixed guidance, blocked tools, slow support and low trust.
!
No AI support modelService desk teams are not ready for AI access, answer-quality or agent incident questions.
!
No evidence or value viewLeaders cannot see what is in use, what is controlled, and what value is being delivered.
◎

Leadership position

Business AI can pursue business value. Technology still needs a clear owner for the AI experience, platform, support, controls, telemetry and agent operating layer.

1

Business case

Reduce friction, lower technology risk, improve licence value and give employees a trusted way to use AI.

2

Establishment logic

Start as a small virtual team with one accountable Technology lead. Formalise only if the 90-day evidence supports scale.

3

Executive ask

Approve a 90-day Technology mandate to build the AI front door, baseline risk, create the service model and prove value.

06 · Establishment plan

90-day evidence sprint

Approve a short mandate first. Use evidence to decide whether to continue, expand or absorb the capability.

Days 1–30: Baseline

  • AI tool inventory
  • Copilot readiness baseline
  • Support model gap review
  • Usage and licence baseline
  • Draft AI service catalogue

Days 31–60: Control & enable

  • Launch AI front door
  • Publish employee guidance and KB
  • Remediate top exposure risks
  • Create agent intake template
  • Define escalation and incident path

Days 61–90: Prove value

  • Publish dashboard
  • Create evidence pack
  • Run 3–5 pilot scenarios
  • Confirm scope and RACI
  • Make continue / scale / stop decision

07 · Everyday work

What the team does in practice

This is the practical operating rhythm that makes the team real, not just a governance label.

Daily

  • Triage AI requests and exceptions
  • Review support tickets and known issues
  • Check urgent access or data exposure concerns
  • Update guidance where employees are confused

Weekly

  • Review new agents and tool requests
  • Prioritise Copilot/data readiness fixes
  • Refresh AI service catalogue
  • Publish support KB updates

Monthly

  • Review adoption, usage and licence value
  • Review incidents, exceptions and risks
  • Update evidence pack
  • Report progress to Technology leadership

Quarterly

  • Validate operating model and team capacity
  • Review maturity against NIST / ISO / internal controls
  • Refresh roadmap and investment ask
  • Decide scale, stop or absorb
08 · Evidence boundary

Sources used

The page avoids unsupported claims and uses public, checkable sources only.

  1. Stanford HAI — 2025 AI Index Report: broad AI adoption, investment, capability and governance trends.
  2. Microsoft — Secure and govern Microsoft 365 Copilot: oversharing remediation, guardrails and AI-related regulatory obligations.
  3. Microsoft — secure governed data foundation for Copilot: IT and security admin guidance for Copilot readiness.
  4. Microsoft Copilot Studio — security and governance: controls and governance for agents.
  5. Microsoft 365 admin center — manage Copilot agents: enable, assign, block, remove and manage agents.
  6. NIST AI Risk Management Framework: govern, map, measure and manage AI risks.
  7. ISO/IEC 42001: AI management system standard for risk and opportunity management.
  8. Australian Government — Policy for responsible use of AI in government: accountable and responsible AI use direction.
  9. Australian Government — National framework for the assurance of AI in government: AI assurance cornerstones and practices.
  10. Model Context Protocol official documentation: MCP as an open standard for connecting AI applications to external systems.

Note: Analyst-style claims or vendor forecast percentages were deliberately not included unless they could be supported from public, checkable sources.
